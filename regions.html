Regional Specialties

Discover the unique culinary traditions of Tunisia's diverse regions

From the Mediterranean coast to the Sahara desert, each Tunisian region has developed its own culinary identity influenced by local geography, history, and available ingredients.

Coastal Region
Capital/Urban
Desert/Oasis

Tunis

Coastal capital with access to both Mediterranean seafood and inland ingredients

The capital and largest city, Tunis is the culinary melting pot of Tunisia. Here you'll find traditional dishes alongside French-influenced cafés and restaurants.

| Signature Dishes

Couscous Royal Brik à l'Oeuf Lablabi Fricassé

The medina of Tunis is a UNESCO World Heritage site with countless food stalls and traditional eateries

Sfax

Major Mediterranean port city surrounded by olive groves

Tunisia's second-largest city and an important port. Sfax is known for its olive oil production and exceptional seafood dishes.

| Signature Dishes

Poisson Complet Sfaxian Couscous Grilled Fish Olive Oil Dishes

Sfax produces some of the world's finest olive oil, with trees dating back centuries

Sousse

Beautiful Mediterranean coastline with fertile agricultural surroundings

A coastal city known as the "Pearl of the Sahel." Sousse combines beach resort culture with authentic Tunisian culinary traditions.

| Signature Dishes

Ojja Merguez Seafood Tagines Grilled Octopus Fish Couscous

The Sousse medina has ancient fortifications and traditional spice markets

Kairouan

Inland city in the central plains, known for date palm oases

The fourth holiest city in Islam and the historical capital. Kairouan is famous for its traditional sweets and pastries.

| Signature Dishes

Makroudh Sweet Couscous Traditional Pastries Honey Desserts

Makroudh from Kairouan is considered the finest in all of Tunisia

Bizerte

Strategic port at the northern tip where the Mediterranean meets Lake Bizerte

The northernmost city in Africa, Bizerte is a fishing port with strong seafood traditions and French colonial influence.

| Signature Dishes

Kafteji Fish Tajine Grilled Sardines Seafood Soups

Bizerte's old port is one of the most picturesque in North Africa

Gabès

Unique coastal oasis where the desert meets the Mediterranean

Known as the "Gateway to the Sahara," Gabès is famous for its coastal oasis, dates, and spicy cuisine.

| Signature Dishes

Chakchouka Date-based Dishes Spicy Stews Oasis Vegetables

Gabès is one of the few places where you can see palm trees on the beach

Djerba

Island paradise with Mediterranean beaches and traditional agriculture

An island in the Gulf of Gabès, Djerba has a unique multicultural heritage reflected in its distinctive cuisine.

| Signature Dishes

Mloukhiya Fish Couscous Djerbian Couscous Seafood Specialties

Djerba is home to one of the oldest Jewish communities in North Africa, influencing local cuisine

Nabeul

Fertile peninsula with citrus groves and proximity to the sea

The capital of the Cap Bon peninsula, famous for pottery, citrus fruits, and harissa production.

| Signature Dishes

Mechouia Salad Harissa-based Dishes Citrus Desserts Pottery-cooked Meals

Nabeul produces much of Tunisia's famous harissa and is known for ceramic tagine pots

Monastir

Beautiful beaches along the Sahel coast

A coastal resort town with a blend of tourism and traditional fishing culture, birthplace of Tunisia's first president.

| Signature Dishes

Kamounia Grilled Fish Seafood Mezze Coastal Salads

The ribat (fortress) of Monastir dates back to 796 AD and overlooks the fishing harbor

Tunisia's Culinary Geography

Northern Coast

Mediterranean influences, abundant seafood, French colonial heritage, and Europe-facing trade routes.

Central Tunisia

Traditional heartland with ancient cities, olive groves, agricultural plains, and preserved culinary heritage.

Southern Regions

Desert oases, date cultivation, spicier cuisine, and Berber influences with Saharan trade connections.
